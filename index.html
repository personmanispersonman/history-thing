Anderson Ruffin Abbott

General

Anderson Ruffin Abbott was the first Black Canadian to be a liscenced family physician.

Abbott's family:

Wilson Abbott

Father, Real Estate Owner

Wilson Ruffin Abbott was a successful businessman as well as a successful landowner, owning close to 50 properties.

Ellen Toyer

Mother

Ellen Toyer Abbott lived in Alabama, owning a grocery store along with Wilson Abbott, but left on a warning that the store were to be pilaged.

Mary Ann Casey Abbott

Wife of Anderson Ruffin Abbott

She married Abbott in 1871 and had a large family of five children.

Birth

Abbott was born into a wealthy family. His parents, Wilson Abbott and Ellen Toyer were from Alabama. They left Alabama when their store was allegedly ransacked, and moved to Toronto. It was there when Wilson started to become a major figure in Toronto, as he began purchasing real estate.

Early Life

When he was a child, he attended the Buxton Mission School in Buxton, a predominantly black rural community. He studied in many schools, both private and public, from the Toronto Academy, to Oberlin College in Ohio, to University College in Toronto. He became a medical student in the Toronto School of medicine studying under Thomas Augusta, another black doctor. In 1861, he became the first Canadian-born black physician by receiving a liscence from the Medical Board of Upper Canada.

Civil War

During the Civil War, Abbott applied to become an assistant surgeon for the Union army, but got rejected. He applied again, and got a job as a civilian surgeon in the U.S. Colored Troops. His work during the war helped his reputation, which made him popular in Washington. So popular in fact, that he cared for Abraham Lincoln when he was dying.

Later Life and Death

After the war, Abbott continued his education, enrolling in more medical classes. In 1871, he got married to Mary Ann Casey. He soon became the president of the Wilberforce Educational Institute, and one of the key objectives he had was to eliminate segregation in schools. In November 1892 he was appointed to aide-de-camp or on the Staff of the Commanding Officers Dept in New York, which is the highest honour that was awarded to an African American in Canada or the US at that time. In 1894, he became more involved with writing. He wrote in many topics, including Darwinism, biology, and medicine.

In 1913, Abbott died at the age of 76. He was buried in the Toronto Necropolis, along with his wife.
